About Me

I am
Yisheng Tang,

a PhD student studying early-modern and modern Japanese literature, with a designated emphasis in
Renaissance and Early Modern Studies at the University of California, Berkeley. I received my B.A. in Comparative Literature and Japanese with a minor in German from the same institution in 2019.

I am currently interested in exploring the question of “friendship” and the history of sociability in nineteenth- and early twentieth-century Japan, as well as the changing boundaries between homoeroticism and friendship, and the notions of theatricality and affect in those periods’ literature.

Authorship and the Depiction of Interiority: The Birth of “Modern Japanese literature”

This project explores the development of notions of authorship and the interiority of fictional characters across the Meiji-Edo divide. It focuses on Edo writers such as Santō Kyōden (1761-1816) and Kyokutei Bakin (1767-1848), and the early Meiji writer Ozaki Kōyō (1868-1903), hoping to developing a new genealogy of the foundation of “modern Japanese literature” in the Meiji period.

Kyokutei Bakin, the Author, and His Work, Fūzoku kingyōden

This project focuses on Bakin’s role as a “conscious” author, his project of naturalizing foreign fictions into Japanese culture, his discussion on language (classical language versus contemporary language, vernacular language versus the language of high register), as well as his experiments with his characters in Fūzoku kingyōden.

Filiation and Affiliation: A Meiji Homosocial Relationship in Natsume Sōseki’s Kokoro

This project aims to explore the wider range of erotic possibilities in Kokoro by paying particular attention to watakushi’s perspective without presupposing the division of homosexual and heterosexual desires. Such a reading will allow us to reconcile the seemingly incompatible schools of reading Kokoro, and provide a new perspective for understanding Kokoro’s place in the Meiji discourse on sexuality.

Quelling Blurring: “The Tale of Shuten Dōji” and Its Interpretations

Instead of creating a sharp division between monsters and humans, “The Tale of Shuten Dōji” in Otogi-zōshi blurs these two identities, creating a new dynamic between them by questioning the very distinctions between these groups established by humans. The new relation between the humans and the monsters then allows us, among other things, to read the story as contemporary commoners’ satire and criticism toward the privileged warrior class.

The Notion of “Closure” in Isabelle de Charriére's Trois femmes

TThe lack of closure of the narrative structure, the enforced unconventional closures on the plot, as well as the intimate homosocial bonds between women, can be understood as signs of resistance to the conventional social order imposed on women. However, the omnipresent male voices that are hidden beneath the dominant female voices in the work suggest a more nuanced relationship among women, men, and the social system they live in.

Public and Private Love: the Representations of Eros in Sappho’s Poetry and Plato’s Symposium

This project focuses on Sappho’s fragment 1 and fragment 94, as well as Phaedrus’ and Pausanias’ speeches in Symposium. It aims to uncover the different models of Love in these poems and accounts, which involve the relationship among the lover, the beloved, and the society they live in. The rhetoric of Love can be used both to deny human subjectivity and to advocate for subjectivity.

Examining Blurring: An Anti-anthropocentric Comparative Study of European Vampirism and Shuten Dōji

In drawing attention to the fact that monsters and humans do become one, and to the ways of how the borders blur and why they blur, “The Tale of Shuten Dōji” in Otogi-zōshi Polidori’s “The Vampyre” offer us insight into questions of desire and legitimacy, but also of the distribution of power in the social and historical contexts the stories were written in.
